The Nuss Procedure

The Nuss procedure involves a concave stainless steel bar that is slipped under the sternum with the assistance of a scope (small camera) using two small incisions on each side of the chest. The bar is then flipped, and the sternum moves to the proper position. (https://www.chop.edu/treatments/nuss-procedure)

The Nuss procedure is a minimally-invasive procedure, invented in 1987 by Dr. Donald Nuss for treating pectus excavatum. He developed it at Children's Hospital of The King's Daughters, in Norfolk, Virginia. The operation typically takes approximately two hours. (wikipedia)

Pectus excavatum can make kids feel self-conscious about the way they look. It can also make it hard for them to exercise and play sports, and cause other health problems. The Nuss procedure can improve kids' emotional and physical well-being. The recovery time can be difficult, but most kids are happy with the results. (https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/nuss-procedure.html)

What Should You Do Before Hand?

If you still want to go through with this procedure. I would suggest:

PBreathing

Get your breathing and exercise under control. This will allow you to calm yourself down when times get rough and painful.

RCommitment

Confirm with yourself that you are 100% committed to this because there is no going back after the procedure is done and the operation has taken place.

ERealization

The realization that this is going to be an uphill battle for a minimum of 3 months after the operation takes place. Every daily task will feel like a goal and mission to accomplish. (your first pushup, crunch/plank and lift will hurt(10/10)

PSupport

Get your support group ready. Family, friends and mentors should be on standby to help you in any case necessary that you will be unable to accomplish by yourself.

Surgrey Day

The day has come, it's official. You are arriving at the hospital filled with mixed emotion about this procedure. Thinking about backing out, but you have already committed this far you decide to see it through to the end.

  • Immediate

    You will be miserable. Hurting, in pain and having constant discomfort. Sleeping will suck, (can not sleep on your side) Eating will be a problem because of the amount of medication you are on. Finally, activity will be at an all time low.

  • 10 Days

    Your first 10 days I advise you to constantly walk, take it slow, don't try anything too rambunctious and get your eating under control. You're mainly going to be on your electronics, social media, and video games.

  • 20 Days

    At the 20 day mark life starts to look up a bit. Not much but a bit. You're going to be in less significant pain. Still on medication though. Your exercise will improve, but not substantially. You won't be able to do a push or anything of that sort just yet. The daily essentials and basics come back with ease.

  • 30 Days

    Finally, you've reached a month or 30 days. Life improves greatly. Mentally you're awesome. Physically your body will hurt a lot. Most of the pain has settled now, and the pills have stopped. Life starts to begin again.

Austin Steffes

Hello, my name is Austin Steffes and I had the nuss procedure when I was 14 years old through 18 years old. Start of high school to the end of high school.

Process

My procedure went rather simplistic. I arrived on scene on June 7th @ Childrens Hospital in Milwaukee, Wisconsin. The team was ready, I was ready. Woke up without any complications. Got out of bed in the matter of 3 hours and started rehab instantly. I only stayed at the hospital for 1 night and left the following day which is unheard of for this procedure. I very much did not like the hospital and wanted to leave as fast as possible. (I fucking hated the yellow room (the color of my room)

Complication

At the 30 day mark, complications arose. I was in more pain than before for no reason at all. After making a critical mistake by putting heat on my incisions they puffed up drastically. They were infected and life wasn't looking good. At that moment, I was rushed to the hospital and put into surgery instantly. The doctors were unsure what happened, why I was in pain and what they had to do to create a solution for me. After an incredible 10 surgeries total, the infection was cleaned and treated for. I stayed in the hospital for a total of 1-2 months throughout this process. My summer was ruined, I was devastated and absolutely hated everyone around me and life in general.

Outcome/Result

If we're being honest with ourselves. I still don't know if this experience was worth the troubles I went through. I became a darker person as a whole. No longer that happy, excited, young man but rather a quiet, calculated and disciplined individual now. I am now 19 years old and still question myself about the procedure. (btw, getting the titanium rod out of your chest is much easier. About a 1 week recovery with little to no pain compared to the titanium rod being implanted into your chest.)
